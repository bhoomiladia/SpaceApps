Landsat Reflectance Information

Landsat Reflectance refers to the measurement of the reflectance of Earth's surface as captured by the Landsat satellites. Reflectance is the ratio of the light energy reflected by a surface to the light energy incident on it. This data is derived from raw satellite observations and is processed to provide physical reflectance values for further analysis.

1. Landsat Missions

Landsat is a series of Earth-observing satellites that have been capturing imagery since 1972. Different versions of the satellites include:

  • Multispectral Scanner (MSS): Landsat 1-5
  • Thematic Mapper (TM): Landsat 4 and 5
  • Enhanced Thematic Mapper Plus (ETM+): Landsat 7
  • Operational Land Imager (OLI): Landsat 8 and 9
  • Thermal Infrared Sensor (TIRS): Landsat 8 and 9

2. Reflectance Types

Top-of-Atmosphere Reflectance (TOA Reflectance)

TOA reflectance is the reflectance measured at the top of Earth's atmosphere, which includes atmospheric influences. It is calculated using the formula:

ρλ = (π * Lλ * d2) / (ESUNλ * cos(θs))

where:

  • ρλ: TOA Reflectance
  • Lλ: Spectral radiance
  • d: Earth-Sun distance (astronomical units)
  • ESUNλ: Solar irradiance
  • θs: Solar zenith angle

Surface Reflectance (SR)

Surface Reflectance represents the reflectance of Earth's surface after correcting for atmospheric effects. This data is essential for environmental analysis. Tools like LEDAPS and LaSRC are used to derive SR from TOA reflectance.

3. Spectral Bands

Landsat sensors capture data in various spectral bands. Some commonly used bands include:

  • Visible Bands (Blue, Green, Red): For vegetation, water, and urban studies.
  • Near-Infrared (NIR): Sensitive to vegetation health.
  • Shortwave Infrared (SWIR): Used for detecting moisture in plants and soil.
  • Thermal Infrared (TIR): Used for temperature analysis.

4. Applications of Landsat Reflectance

Landsat reflectance data is widely used in various fields, including:

  • Vegetation Indices: Such as NDVI, which measure vegetation health.
  • Water Quality and Land Use: Monitoring changes in water bodies, deforestation, and urbanization.
  • Climate Studies: Analyzing long-term environmental changes like desertification and ice melt.

5. Landsat Data Processing

To analyze reflectance data, the raw satellite imagery undergoes the following steps:

  • Radiometric Calibration: Conversion of raw digital numbers (DNs) into radiance.
  • TOA Reflectance Conversion: Conversion from radiance to TOA reflectance.
  • Surface Reflectance Estimation: Applying atmospheric corrections for surface reflectance.

6. Tools for Analyzing Landsat Reflectance

Common tools for analyzing Landsat data include:

  • Google Earth Engine (GEE): For large-scale data processing.
  • QGIS/ArcGIS: GIS tools for importing and analyzing imagery.
  • Python Libraries: Such as rasterio, geopandas, and earthpy for programmatic analysis.
